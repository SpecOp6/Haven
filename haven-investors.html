A growing crisis with no predictive solution

Over 10,000 Americans turn 65 every day. The aging population is accelerating, and fall-related injuries and deaths are rising faster than the population itself.

Today's fall detection products — wearable pendants, camera systems, smart watches — share a fundamental limitation: they are reactive. They detect impact after it happens. By then, the damage is done.

Research shows that gait abnormalities are established predictors of fall risk. Subtle changes in walking speed, stride length, and balance appear weeks or months before a fall occurs. The technology to detect these changes contactlessly exists — it just hasn't been commercialized.

Until now.

Stride

Predictive gait analysis

A small radar sensor — the size of a smoke detector — sends invisible radio waves that bounce off the body. It measures walking speed, step length, gait symmetry, and balance with clinical precision. Then it tracks changes over time.

  • 96–98% accuracy in published research
  • Builds personal gait baselines
  • Detects degradation before it's visible
  • Privacy-preserving by design
The moat
From detection to prediction
Haven doesn't just catch falls — it catches the warning signs weeks before they happen.

Weeks 1–4: Learn the baseline

Stride passively observes how each person walks — speed, cadence, symmetry, arm swing — and builds a personal gait fingerprint unique to them.

Ongoing: Continuous, invisible monitoring

Every walk through the home is compared against the baseline. No cameras. No wearables. The resident doesn't know the system is there.

Early warning: Gait degradation detected

Stride length shortens by 8%. Walking speed drops 0.15 m/s over three weeks. Left-right asymmetry increases. Too subtle for clinical observation — but Stride catches it.

Intervention: The fall never happens

Beacon alerts the caregiver or clinician. Physical therapy is ordered. Medication is reviewed. Home modifications are made. The ER visit that would have cost $18,658 never occurs.

Regulatory & reimbursement
The path is proven
Haven doesn't need to pioneer a regulatory pathway. A competitor already cleared it.
FDA pathway

510(k) substantial equivalence

Xandar Kardian's XK300 — a radar-based, contactless vital sign monitor — received FDA 510(k) clearance as a Class II medical device in April 2021. Haven's Stride engine uses a related RF modality for a complementary application, positioning it for clearance through the same substantial equivalence pathway.

Xandar Kardian holds 32 patents granted, 11 pending, and has deployed across 50+ healthcare facilities in the United States.

Reimbursement

Medicare CPT Code 99454

The XK300 already qualifies for Medicare reimbursement under CPT Code 99454 (Remote Patient Monitoring). Once Haven achieves 510(k) clearance, the same reimbursement code applies — meaning the customer doesn't pay out of pocket. Medicare does.

In 2020, Medicare paid $53.3 billion of the $80 billion in fall-related healthcare costs. Haven aligns with Medicare's structural incentive to fund prevention over treatment.
